Why 95% of agency websites look identical.

Design Theory Web Design Agency Brand Identity

Open thirty agency websites in thirty tabs. I'll wait. What you'll find — predictably, depressingly — is an almost perfect convergence: the same Inter or Space Grotesk typeface, the same purple-to-blue gradient on the hero, the same three-column service cards with rounded corners, the same client logos scrolling in a marquee strip. Different agencies. Different cities. Different price points. Same website.

This isn't a coincidence. It's the logical output of a system that rewards speed of execution over depth of thinking. And if you're an agency owner reading this on your own homepage right now, there's a non-trivial chance your site is one of them.

The Figma Template Economy

The rise of UI kit marketplaces — Figma Community, UI8, Gumroad — has done something genuinely useful for the industry: it democratized the ability to build professional-looking interfaces quickly. But it's also created a parallel problem. When everyone draws from the same well, they produce water that tastes the same.

The modern agency website workflow often looks like this: buy a $49 Figma template, swap the colors to match the brand, swap the copy, swap the images, publish. The resulting site feels professional enough to not embarrass, premium enough to not concern. But it's fundamentally anonymous. It could belong to anyone. That's not a feature. That's a crisis.

"When your website could belong to any agency, it's doing active work against you. The most memorable brand statement is one that could only come from you."

Why Visual Homogeneity Is a Business Problem

You might argue: clients care about results, not aesthetics. They care about case studies, testimonials, process. If the work is good, the website is secondary.

This is true and false simultaneously. The work gets you shortlisted. The website determines whether they believe you before they've seen the work. Within the first twelve seconds of a visit, a potential client has formed an impression of your competence, your taste, and your character. They haven't read a word. They've only felt something. If what they've felt is familiar — if your site reads as "another agency" — you've forfeited the opportunity to stand out before the conversation begins.

The Three Culprits

There are three forces driving this homogeneity, and they're worth naming explicitly before we can escape them.

  1. The Comfort of Convention. Familiar layouts reduce friction. The hero → services → work → testimonials → CTA structure is ubiquitous because it works well enough. The problem is that "works" and "differentiates" are not the same thing. A structure that everyone uses communicates nothing about you specifically.

  2. The Speed Imperative. Agency websites are often the project that gets the least strategic attention. You're busy building other people's work. Your own site gets rebuilt in a weekend, using the fastest available tools. Speed and originality are not enemies — but they require discipline to coexist.

  3. The Fear of Misreading. Agencies are terrified of alienating clients who might not understand a bold visual choice. So they retreat to the safe middle — the design language that offends no one, surprises no one, and stays with no one.

/* The most common hero in 2026 */
.hero {
  background: linear-gradient(135deg, #6B46F6, #2D9CDB);
  font-family: 'Inter', sans-serif; /* always Inter */
  border-radius: 24px; /* always rounded */
}

/* What it communicates: nothing about you */

The Escape

Differentiation doesn't require maximalism. It doesn't require a site that looks like a Dadaist art piece. It requires one committed, specific design decision that could only belong to you — and then executing that decision with complete confidence.

Consider what makes these approaches memorable:

  • A studio that uses a single serif typeface at 200px and nothing else for the hero — the restraint is the statement.
  • An agency that uses only their own photography, color-graded to a specific film stock — visual consistency as brand language.
  • A consultancy whose entire site is typeset in a custom monospace font with zero images — the medium communicates precision.

None of these are complicated. Each one is a choice — a clear answer to the question: what does this look like if it could only be ours?

"The most dangerous question in design isn't 'will they understand it?' It's 'could this belong to anyone else?'"

In Practice: Five Starting Points

If you're sitting with a site that looks like everyone else's, here's where to begin. Not a redesign — a rethinking:

  1. Audit your font stack. If you're using Inter, Space Grotesk, or Nunito — ask yourself why. Not to abandon them, but to confirm the choice is intentional. If the answer is "it was the default," that's where to start.

  2. Identify one section to break. Not every section needs to be unusual. But one should do something that stops scrolling. An unexpected layout, a surprising proportion, a typographic choice that earns a second look.

  3. Write a visual brief for yourself. Three adjectives. Not "modern, minimal, clean" — those describe half the internet. Try "austere, warm, direct" or "cinematic, tactile, obsessive." The specificity forces real design decisions.

  4. Constrain your color palette to two. A background and a single accent. No gradients. No palette of six. Constraint is clarity. Clarity is character.

  5. Treat motion as editorial punctuation. One entrance animation, used consistently. Not a carousel. Not parallax on everything. One thing that reveals your content with intention.

Final Thought

The agencies doing the most interesting work in the world right now are not the ones with the most features on their site. They're the ones whose site feels like a position — a declaration of what they stand for and, just as importantly, what they refuse to do.

Anonymous is not safe. Anonymous is invisible. In a market where every agency claims to be different, the ones who prove it visually — before a single word is read — are the ones who get the call.

The template economy will keep producing the same output. The question is whether you're willing to step outside it.
